Past tense parameters

proximity

  • proximal: a past event proximal to present
  • non-proximal: a past event no longer considered recent

confirmativity

  • confirmative: an event for which the speaker has direct evidence
  • non-confirmative: an event for which the speaker feels surprise, doubt, or lack of direct evidence
    • i.e., includes evidential, dubitative, mirativity

Past tense organisation: Comparison

Comparison of languages

  • Three-way systems: Middle Mongol, Old Turkic, non-CA Turkic
  • Four-way systems: CA Turkic, Khalkha

Similarities across languages with four-way systems

  • past tense has 2 two-way distinctions: ±proximal, ±confirmative
  • parallels in non-confirmative forms
    • proximal — suppletion: -lAA / -jee, -DI / -(I)ptIr
    • non-proximal — non-confirmative based on confirmative:
      -GAn + eken, -sAŋ + yum baina
    • non-proximal historically formed based on participle
  • some similarities noticed by Brosig (2014)

Negation: Comparison

Comparison of languages

  • Single-strategy systems: Middle Mongol, Old Turkic, CA Turkic
  • Multi-strategy systems: CA Turkic, Khalkha

Similarities across multi-strategy systems

  • Suppletion (in 1 to 3 cells): -lAA / -sAŋ-güi, -DI / -GAn joq
    (cf. -mA-DI, ese _-jUGU)
  • Proximal confirmative formed on non-proximal confirmative base:
    -sAŋ-güi, -GAn joq
  • morpheme order in novel negation forms:
    tense + neg (+ non-confirmativity):
    -GAn emes eken, -(G)AA-güi baina
    (cf. conservative systems: -mA-GAn ikän, ese _-jUGU)
  • some similarities noticed by Washington (2023)

Summary

Observations

  • Khalkha and CA Turkic past tense systems exhibit many similarities
    • Organisation: four-way ±confirmative, ±proximal
    • Negation: suppletion, formation, morpheme order
  • not present in older Mongolic or Turkic languages
  • not present in other modern Turkic languages

Our proposal

  • Parallels in the systems are the result of language contact
  • Likely involved sustained period of bilingualism
  • Borrowing in one or the other direction possible, but no obvious source
  • Systems may themselves be the product of contact

Wrapping up

Conclusions

  • Predecessors of Khalkha and Central Asian Turkic shared a period of intense contact (cf. Washington, 2023)
  • The languages had a huge influence on one another
  • Past tense systems were affected

Future work

  • Look at specific trajectories from one system to the other:
    • Use of participles as base for novel forms
    • Intermediate historical languages: Chaghatay, Khwarezmian, etc.
  • Investigate variation, additional forms:
    • Khalkha -v
    • Tatar -GAn-[person] ikän ~ -GAn ikän-[person]
    • Kazakh/Kyrgyz -MA-DI/-BA-DI (vs. -GAn joq)
  • Compare to more languages:
    • Siberian Turkic, other Mongolic languages
    • Third-party source for similarities, lack thereof?
